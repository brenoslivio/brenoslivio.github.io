Who Am I?

I am a double-degree PhD student at the Institute of Mathematics and Computer Sciences (ICMC) of the University of São Paulo (USP) and the University of Leipzig, researching pattern recognition in biological sequences using alignment-free methods such as language models. My work focuses on developing computational approaches to analyze complex biological data, bridging machine learning and bioinformatics.

As part of my doctoral research, I am also a Research Assistant at the Helmholtz Centre for Environmental Research (UFZ) in the Microbial Data Science Group headed by Dr. Ulisses da Rocha (Department of Computational Biology and Chemistry). Here, I apply and extend my PhD methods to study microbial systems, contributing to large-scale environmental data analysis and advancing tools for sequence-based discovery.

Before my PhD, I completed my BSc in Computer Science at ICMC-USP, where I first explored machine-learning applications in bioinformatics. During this time, I had the privilege of working under the guidance of Prof. Dr. André de Carvalho, who played a pivotal role in shaping my research interests. Now, as a PhD student, I am fortunate to continue this academic journey with him as one of my co-advisors, alongside Prof. Dr. Peter Stadler from the University of Leipzig.

Research Interests

Since High School, where I developed a simple Chatbot for assistance in interventions with patients with Asperger's Syndrome, I tried to use computer science for social good. I continued doing this in college, where my interests in biology and nutrition led me to try to understand disorders such as Autism Spectrum and Bipolar Disorder through the lens of machine learning and bioinformatics.

Since 2021, I have been continuing to work on computer science for social good by employing methods for democratizing machine learning, such as using automated machine learning (AutoML), which can help non-experts to use machine learning.

My current research interests, in a more technical version:

I am interested in developing alignment-free computational approaches, particularly machine learning-based methods to analyze biological sequences that resist traditional annotation due to high divergence and remote homology. By leveraging language models to extract evolutionary and ecological features, I aim to build nature-inspired machine-learning frameworks for the discovery of novel enzymes.

Now, in a less technical version that I could explain to my mom and impress her:

Proteins are molecules that perform many essential functions in the body, such as building tissues and regulating processes. Some researchers like to call proteins with unknown functions as being "functional dark matter" (although some are pretty much against using the term "dark matter").

Even with databases containing billions of proteins, those proteins are not considered similar to those with functions in the databases, so we cannot adequately characterize them. The problem with the methods that do this comparison is that they can be prone to errors.

These methods compare protein sequences to find similarities, but they can miss highly divergent sequences, which are proteins that have changed so much over time that their shared origins are hard to detect. They also struggle with remote homologs: distantly related proteins with the same evolutionary ancestor but very different sequences. Because these methods rely on apparent similarities, many important but deeply evolved proteins remain unknown, leaving gaps in our understanding of "functional dark matter."

Just as language models, like the one used by ChatGPT, can learn patterns in words in a sentence, they can also learn patterns in amino acids of proteins, finding hidden patterns in the "language of life" such evolutionary and ecological information. I believe language models can be key to unlocking nature's hidden biochemical potential by finding novel enzymes, discovering new enzymes that could revolutionize medicine, industry, and sustainability.
